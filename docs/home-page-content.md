# RanVault Website Strategy & Structure

Working document — source of truth for site structure, navigation, and hero section before copywriting begins.

---

## 1. Goal of the Page

Inform visitors clearly and guide them toward one action: **booking a free security assessment.** Everything on the page should serve that single goal — no competing CTAs, no distractions.

---

## 2. What Makes a Cybersecurity Landing Page Successful (10 Factors)

Research-backed, applied to RanVault's ICP (non-technical clinic owners/administrators, 30–100 staff, cautious buyers):

1. **10-second clarity.** Visitors decide whether to stay within 10–20 seconds. The hero must say "this is for you" instantly — no jargon, no ambiguity.
2. **Pain-first headline, not product-first.** The headline should name the buyer's problem or desired outcome, not describe the company's features.
3. **Trust signals above the fold.** Cybersecurity buyers need 2–3x more trust signals than typical B2B buyers before converting. HIPAA, 42 CFR Part 2, and "U.S.-Based" should be visible early.
4. **One conversion goal per page.** A single primary CTA, repeated; no competing actions splitting attention.
5. **The hero must answer 3 questions at once:** Who is this for? What changes for them? What's the next step?
6. **Proof mapped to specific objections**, not a generic testimonial block — RanVault's existing battlecards (objection → rebuttal) are ideal raw material for this.
7. **Minimal form friction.** 3 fields max for the first conversion point (name, work email, clinic name) — longer forms convert significantly worse.
8. **Mid-page CTAs**, not just top and bottom — most visitors won't scroll to the end.
9. **Mobile-first design.** Most B2B buyers research on mobile; layout, tap targets, and trust signals must hold up on small screens.
10. **Multi-stakeholder messaging.** The page needs to reassure both the owner (risk/reputation) and the office manager/admin (operational simplicity, compliance) — RanVault's buyer group includes both.

**Reality check:** Cybersecurity has one of the lowest B2B conversion benchmarks (~1%) because buyers research extensively before acting. The page's job isn't to "sell" in one visit — it's to build enough trust that a cautious, non-technical buyer takes the low-commitment next step (book a free assessment).

## 13. FAQ / Objections

Built from existing sales battlecards. Use real objection/rebuttal pairs from internal sales material rather than generic FAQ content. Suggested starter questions (replace/expand using actual battlecard content):

1. **"We're too small to be a target."** — Smaller clinics are increasingly targeted because attackers know they have weaker defenses.
2. **"We already have an IT provider."** — General IT providers handle hardware and support — they're not the same as dedicated security and compliance specialists.
3. **"Isn't this going to be expensive?"** — RanVault is built specifically to be affordable for independent clinics, not priced like enterprise security.
4. **"How is this different from hiring in-house?"** — You get senior-level security expertise without the cost of a full-time hire.

**Headline options considered (for reference):**
1. *Healthcare Secured. Always Assured.* — tagline-based, brand-consistent
2. *Don't Let a Data Breach Define Your Clinic.* — pain-first, but risks feeling alarmist
3. ✅ **Focus on Patients. We'll Handle Security.** — outcome-first, calm tone — **SELECTED**
4. *HIPAA Compliance, Without the Headache.* — compliance-first, narrower audience

### Notes on the original draft
- **"HIPPA" → "HIPAA"** — critical fix; a typo here undermines the entire compliance trust proposition.
- Avoid stacking three separate headline ideas ("Behavioral Health Secured. Always Assured" / "Cybersecurity for Behavioral Health Clinics" / "Trusted Quality. Smart Pricing") into one block — each competes for the same 10-second attention window. Split into eyebrow / headline / subheadline / trust-strip roles instead.
- "Trusted Quality. Smart Pricing" works well as a trust-strip item or small line near the CTA, not as a third headline.
